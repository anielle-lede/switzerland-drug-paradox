Data Analysis · Drug Policy

Swiss drug crime is falling, even as cocaine remains everywhere.

Cocaine residues remain among Europe's highest. Yet drug offences have almost halved — and the people behind them are getting older.

By Anielle Peterhans

In European wastewater measurements, Switzerland consistently ranks near the top for cocaine residues — alongside countries like Belgium, the Netherlands and Spain. Wastewater studies have repeatedly identified Switzerland as one of Europe's higher-consuming countries for cocaine. Researchers reported above-average levels in Swiss cities as early as 2014, and recent analyses continue to place several Swiss cities among Europe's cocaine hotspots. Yet in Swiss police statistics, drug-related offences have fallen by nearly half over the past 15 years.

How can a country rank among Europe's highest cocaine consumers while recording dramatically fewer drug offences?

What wastewater tells us that arrests cannot

Wastewater analysis is one of the most direct windows into drug consumption that researchers have. Instead of relying on police seizures or self-reported surveys — both of which carry obvious biases — scientists measure drug metabolites in sewage systems and compare average daily loads across cities.

How it works When the body processes a drug, it excretes metabolites — chemical traces that pass into the sewage system. Researchers sample wastewater at treatment plants and measure these residues, then normalise the figures by population to allow comparisons across sites and countries.

In the latest dataset from the European Union Drugs Agency, Switzerland ranks eighth out of 41 participating countries for cocaine residues — placing it squarely in the same tier as Belgium, the Netherlands and Spain.

Source: European Union Drugs Agency wastewater analysis. Figures show average daily loads of cocaine metabolites per 1,000 inhabitants.

Swiss cities have very different drug profiles

The national ranking is only part of the picture. When you look at individual Swiss cities, the variation is striking — and cocaine is not the only substance worth examining.

The geographic spread matters too. Cocaine signals are highest in larger urban centres — a pattern visible across Europe, where cities tend to show higher consumption than rural areas.

Bubble size reflects average daily cocaine load per 1,000 inhabitants. Source: European Union Drugs Agency.

Police records tell a different story

Turn from the sewers to the police stations, and Switzerland looks like a very different country.

According to Swiss Federal Statistical Office data, police recorded around 44,000 drug-related offences in 2025. In 2009, the figure stood at more than 85,000. That is a fall of roughly 48 percent — sustained over more than a decade, even as wastewater data shows consumption holding steady or rising.

Police-recorded drug offences, Switzerland 2009–2025. Source: Swiss Federal Statistical Office.

The sharpest declines are in offences linked to personal possession and consumption — categories most directly shaped by enforcement priorities. This is a crucial clue: it suggests the drop reflects a change in how Swiss authorities police drug use, not necessarily how much drug use is happening.

"The two datasets measure different things. Wastewater captures what people consume. Police statistics capture what authorities choose to pursue."

The typical drug suspect has changed

The most revealing shift in the police data is not the total number of offences, but who is being recorded.

In 2009, people aged 20 to 24 made up nearly a quarter of all suspected drug offenders. By 2025, their share had fallen to around 15 percent. Over the same period, the share of suspects aged 50 to 59 more than tripled — rising from under 2 percent to nearly 8 percent.

Share of suspected drug offenders by age group, Switzerland 2009–2025. Source: Swiss Federal Statistical Office.

This is not simply an ageing population effect. The shift is too pronounced, and too concentrated in the police-recorded data, to be explained by demographics alone. It likely reflects a combination of factors: changing enforcement patterns, generational differences in drug use, and possibly a shift in how younger people's drug activity comes to police attention — or doesn't.

Reading the paradox

Put the two datasets side by side and the paradox sharpens. Wastewater signals remain high — Switzerland is still one of Europe's larger cocaine-consuming countries by this measure. But the criminal justice footprint of that consumption has contracted substantially.

What the data cannot tell us is why, with any certainty. Decriminalisation trends, shifting enforcement resources, changes in how offences are classified, a genuine reduction in youth drug use — any or all of these could be contributing.
